Testimony And Oral History

01:14

"Racism is maybe the worst heritage that we have from the colonial society. It is a way of considering people by the color of their skin, more than what we have inside. It is more about the external appearance than what we have as humans. And it is true that racism has changed in a certain sense. If before, racism justify that the men of African origin was enslaved, and we used to say yes because it did not have certain features. As a consequence, it was supposed to be right in that situation. Later, with the formation of our Republic, racism was not the same to justify that the African men were slaves, but second-class citizens. For instance, this person cannot join the Navy—not because he is Black, mind you, but because—as you know—one must be "respectable"; one must have the right family background and a solid income. And naturally, he doesn't fit the bill. Instead, the attitude is: "Well, you're Black—you're fine for sports, or maybe for dancing." Thus, racism has shifted somewhat in its tone and manner of expression, but it remains fundamentally unchanged in its essence. Consequently, we are still effectively dragging this legacy behind us—because this mentality persisted, because the old hacienda system endured, and because we continue to inhabit the poorest areas, leaving our social status essentially unchanged. In reality, to speak of the poor in Peru is, by and large, to speak of Black people; there is a very strong correlation between poverty and race in our country." Jose "Pepe" Luciano was an activist and Afro-Peruvian reseacher about the social conditions of Black people in Peru. In this phrase, Pepe bring attention to the changes on racism from the colonial period to the early 90s.

"I am Black, and mind you, that feminine ending changes everything. I am a woman, and being a woman entails a specific kind of struggle in relation to the broader social group primarily due to how the term itself is perceived by society as a whole. In order to reach a certain level, I have to spend an immense amount of time relentlessly pushing at things until people finally grasp that I am capable of doing the job because I actually know how to do it. First, I have to make others look past the color of my skin; and second, I have to make them look past my gender. People simply refuse to accept me for who I am—plain and simple. That is to say, quite simply, a woman cannot give orders to a group of men." Susana reveals the intersection of gender and race and how she has to navigate the world that reduces her to her gender and race

Testimony And Oral History

03:54

"Indeed, racism does exist in Peru—and it exists because our avenues for advancement are limited. To gain access to any given sphere, we are regularly compelled to prove that we are superior to everyone else. That is to say, while everyone must exert effort to access something or acquire a specific objective—as doing so always requires work—the effort *we* must expend, at any level, invariably has to exceed that of the average person. In short, opportunities are scarce precisely *because* of racism. For instance, if a white person seeks employment at a company, they are accepted; a Black person, however, might not be. This is a frequent occurrence—let's take, for example, a job application submitted to a government agency: people of color face discrimination. Ultimately, I believe that equal opportunities do not exist for people of color and white people; for the former, every opportunity—and everything they have had to accomplish—has required far more effort than it has for anyone else." Susana Matute Charun, a politician and activist, reflects on the implications that racism has in limiting the opportunities of Black people.
